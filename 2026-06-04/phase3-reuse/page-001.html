Key Takeaways

  1. Don't rebuild payment execution — claims-payment-disbursement-service already handles BACS/SEPA/ACH/cheque/wallet with dual-control approval. We reconcile its outputs, not replace them.

  2. Don't rebuild payment history — claims-status-api already exposes GET /claims/{ref}/payments. We consume this as the "expected" side.

  3. Audit logging is mandatory, not optional — the audit-log-api description explicitly states "All Parasol systems must submit." Our reconciliation events (matches, discrepancies, exceptions) must go here.

  4. Fraud scoring is available for payment transactions — POST /scores/payment on the fraud-score-api lets us flag suspicious discrepancies during reconciliation, not just at disbursement time.

  5. Cross-team dependency — we'll need to coordinate with at least 3 teams: claims-engineering (our own), billing-payments-engineering (premium-payment-api), and compliance-engineering (audit-log-api).

★ Insight ───────────────────────────────────── Why this catalog-driven discovery matters for the reconciliation service: 1. The "bank" side vs "book" side — reconciliation matches what the bank says happened (disbursement-service records) against what the system says should have happened (settlement-service instructions). Both already exist as separate services — our job is to compare, not duplicate. 2. The premium-payment-api is in a different system (payment-processing-system) from our claims payment system. This cross-system boundary means premium refunds or overpayment credits flow through a different team's API — a real integration complexity to design for. 3. The audit-log-api's append-only design (POST returns 204, no update/delete endpoints) means reconciliation discrepancies become an immutable record — exactly what a regulator wants to see. ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
